Identifying Policy Options

Does the Problem Have Potential Policy Solutions?

Tracking Down Previous Work

Chances are the problem you are studying is not unique and, at some point, other policy analysts have tackled it or a similar problem. A good strategy is to track down previous work to see if existing policy options can be applied to your current problem. To find potential policy solutions for the problem, you could include in your review a search of:

  • Policy briefs and documents from CDC, professional associations, stakeholders, peer-reviewed publications or policy organizations.
  • “Best practices” or “promising practices” from CDC and other states or locations. If there are policy solutions that have been successful in other locations, you need to understand what elements made them successful. Was there something context specific that enabled that policy, or could it be applied in other settings?
  • Editorials/discussion pieces by CDC and professional associations.

Although you may study policy options from professional associations and policy organizations, some may be considered advocacy groups. You would need to carefully evaluate materials from organizations that are considered advocacy groups to determine their suitability for use.
